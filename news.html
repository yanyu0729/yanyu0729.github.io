March

New Article Published

Indicators are powerful tools for guiding nutrient management, but they need to be defined transparently and applied within appropriate contexts. In this study, we developed a transparent framework to more consistently evaluate manure nitrogen recycling potential across the United States.

Our framework includes five key metrics for estimating recoverable manure nitrogen and crop nitrogen demand under both current and future conditions. The results highlight that improving manure recycling is not simply about replacing synthetic fertilizer, but about building a more circular nutrient system connecting livestock production, crops, waste streams, energy systems, and policy.

I am very grateful to my mentors and collaborators for their guidance and support throughout this journey.

December

Presented Two Posters at AGU

In December 2025, I attended the AGU Annual Meeting and presented two posters. One poster focused on integrating systems thinking into nitrogen management using insights from quantifying decadal U.S. nitrogen budget in 2012, 2017, and 2022. The other presented a framework for assessing manure nitrogen balance and recycling potential under current and future conditions.

The meeting provided a valuable opportunity to share my work, receive feedback, and connect with researchers working across Earth, environmental, and agricultural sciences.

June–July

Field Research at USDA Jornada Experimental Range

Although much of my research involves systems modeling, spatial analysis, and working with large datasets, meaningful science begins with understanding where those data originate.

During the summer of 2025, I joined colleagues at the USDA Agricultural Research Service's Jornada Experimental Range in southern New Mexico to learn firsthand how manure management and agricultural field measurements are conducted.

Spending time in the field provided valuable perspective on the observations, sampling, and long-term monitoring efforts that underpin many of the datasets used in agricultural and environmental research.

This experience reminded me that robust data depend on dedicated field scientists and technicians whose work often happens behind the scenes. Connecting field observations with modeling and data analysis is essential for developing practical, science-based solutions for sustainable nutrient management.
